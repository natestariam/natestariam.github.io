01 · How I see On

A brand that already runs
on craft. AI should make it
feel inevitable.

On isn't a tech company trying to add craft. It's a craft company that has quietly built one of the most technically ambitious supply chains in sportswear — LightSpray, CloudTec, 32 robots spraying a shoe upper in three minutes. The story is already radical. The question is what AI changes about how runners, retail partners, and the wider community experience it.

I believe the AI Lab's role isn't to layer AI features onto the product roadmap. It's to protect On's most valuable asset — its sense of taste — while using AI to deepen the relationship between the brand and its community at every touchpoint: the run, the store, the website, the recovery, the next pair.

"The best AI products don't feel like AI. They feel like the brand, turned up. That's what the Lab should be aiming for — not innovation as novelty, but innovation as intimacy."

The thread · Hyperpersonalisation

Not a configuration.
A conversation.

Every other brand treats personalisation as something you do at purchase — pick a colour, stitch your name, go. That's not personalisation. That's gift-wrap. The real opportunity is to design a loop where the shoe and the runner are in continuous dialogue, and every pair is a rendering of what On has learned about that person since the last one.

"Hyperpersonalisation isn't a feature you ship once. It's a relationship you design for years."

01
Identity
A persistent digital twin — foot, gait, biomechanics, history. Not a profile. A living model.
02
Expression
Every pair is a rendering of that twin at a moment. No two runners get the same shoe. No two pairs from the same runner either.
03
Adaptation
Sensors in the shoe feed wear and gait data back to the twin. Over 200km, it knows you better than you know you.
04
Context
The same runner gets different shoes for different moments. Race-day, recovery, jet-lagged travel. Personal × contextual.
Three scenarios that prove the loop
Scenario A
The race-day shoe
Friday night. Maya's marathon is Sunday. Her AI generates a one-off race shoe from the course elevation, Sunday's 15°C forecast, 12 weeks of training, and her fatigue pattern at km 30+. Sprayed overnight. At her hotel Saturday.
"A shoe nobody else will ever own."
Scenario B
The bad-day shoe
She's slept poorly three nights running. Her HRV is low. Her next refresh's midsole is composed with 12% more cushioning — automatically. She doesn't know it. The shoe is softer because she needs softer.

She's tweaked her Achilles. Her physio notes flow into her twin (with her permission). The next refresh reinforces the heel counter and drops the offset by 2mm. The shoe heals with her.

A lineage · Fashion × Performance

The spray has
history.

On didn't invent the theatre of spraying clothes onto bodies — fashion did. The difference is that fashion has treated it as spectacle, a one-night show. On has the manufacturing technology to make it a daily, scalable act. That's a rare place to stand.

The sweet spot the Lab should design for isn't "AI for sportswear." It's the much rarer overlap where high fashion's obsession with craft as performance meets high performance's obsession with craft as advantage. On already lives there. The question is how visible we make it.

The opportunity
Lab Concept · 18 months out
The Continuous Pair.

You don't buy shoes anymore — you keep a pair. Every 200km, you return to an Atelier. The upper dissolves back into material. A new one is sprayed in three minutes, tuned to how you just ran. The midsole is refreshed on a longer cycle. The shoe never dies. It evolves.

What you own at year one is measurably different from what you bought at year zero — softer here, firmer there, shaped by your actual runs. Zero waste by design. Your shoe becomes a record of your running life.

What Rolls-Royce did to aircraft engines ("power by the hour"), On could do to footwear: the product as an ongoing relationship, not a transaction.

The bigger strategic bet · Beyond the run

On isn't a running
brand. Not anymore.

Here's an uncomfortable truth the Lab should confront early: the majority of On customers don't run in On shoes. They wear them to brunch, to the airport, to the office, to yoga. They're affluent, often over 40, and they buy Cloud and Cloud X as lifestyle objects that happen to be technically excellent. The brand talks about marathons. The cash register tells a different story.

This isn't a problem. It's an opportunity On is under-exploiting. The Lab's job is to design for the runners On actually has, not just the ones it markets to — and to build the verticals that compete where On doesn't yet: trail, training, studio, and the high-fashion crossover where Arc'teryx, Lululemon, Alo, and Loro Piana are quietly eating lunch.

The strategic reframe
On isn't the Swiss Nike. It should become the Loro Piana of moving bodies.

Quiet luxury brands own wardrobe ritual, material obsession, and generational wear. Technical brands own performance and specificity. On already has both instincts — LightSpray is craft-obsession at scale, CloudTec is technical specificity. The Lab's strategic job is to merge these into a clear positioning: sportswear that's worn with intent, not just aspiration. And to give the Coffee Runner a dedicated product line without apologising for it.

The sport-geek lever · The Athlete Protocol

Stop using Federer
as a face.

On has something almost no other sportswear brand does: genuine, deep, ongoing relationships with elite athletes. Roger Federer as a co-founder. The entire OAC roster — Hellen Obiri, Yared Nuguse, George Beamish. Marathon winners wearing On at the Olympics. This is credibility most brands would spend a decade trying to manufacture.

And On markets it the way everyone else does. Billboards, hero films, Instagram. The Lab's opportunity is to turn these relationships into something that marketing can't touch — a layer of the product itself. Not "inspired by the pros." Literally configured by them. That's Experience Design, not brand.

The reframe
Athletes aren't
ambassadors.
They're source code.

Federer's hands. Hellen Obiri's gait. Yared Nuguse's race-pace strategy. These aren't content. They're inputs — biomechanical, tactical, personal — that can flow directly into the product experience for everyone else. The athlete's expertise becomes infrastructure.

"Stop using Federer as a face. Start using him as source code."

The adjacent vertical

Tennis is the
obvious next door.

Federer isn't just a face — he's the most credible tennis voice alive, and On hasn't built a proper tennis product. On The Roger is a lifestyle sneaker, not a court shoe. Meanwhile the tennis footwear category is dominated by brands with half his credibility. The Lab's move: scope a real tennis line shaped by Federer's camp — court shoes, apparel, training tools — and design the experience around making his tactical intelligence available to amateur players through the app. Instant credibility, new vertical, real product.

The proposition for the AI Lab
Marketing sells stories about athletes.
The Lab should build products informed by athletes.

The distinction is everything. Stories live on billboards for a month. Products informed by athletes live on feet for years — and pay dividends in trust, authenticity, and the one thing On has more of than any competitor: credible sporting lineage. The Head of Experience Design's remit is to turn that lineage into an architectural feature of the product ecosystem, not a marketing asset. That's what separates the Lab from the brand team.

The uncomfortable truth · The Taste Reset

On has a perception
problem. Not a
product problem.

There's a pattern worth saying aloud because most of the room will already feel it: On's cultural stock has slipped. The Zendaya commercial run before Drama felt like a brand borrowing taste it hadn't earned — the cultural-fashion audience clocked it immediately. The Cloud X is now so ubiquitous it's become the shoe of choice for people the fashion crowd specifically wants to not be mistaken for. And the most telling signal: among fashion-literate buyers, On's apparel is doing the heavy cultural lifting — the footwear is being tolerated, not celebrated.

This isn't a brand team problem. You can't fix it with better ads. It's a product, ritual, and distribution problem — which means it's exactly where the Lab should step in. The way out is to design the objects, moments, and access points that re-earn credibility from the people who set taste for everyone else.

Four symptoms, named honestly
SYMPTOM 01
The Zendaya era
A-list celebrity marketing to fashion buyers who can smell it. Borrowed credibility landed as desperation, not cultural alignment. The Challengers tie-in read as a brand trying to be where taste is, not a brand that is.
Signal: fashion press went quiet. Stan Twitter didn't bite.
SYMPTOM 02
Cloud saturation
The Cloud and Cloud X are now the default comfort shoe of the mass affluent. When everyone in the airport lounge is wearing the same thing, the people who notice will start looking elsewhere. Ubiquity has outpaced intent.
Signal: "the new white sneaker that isn't cool anymore."
SYMPTOM 03
Apparel carries water
The most fashion-credible thing about On right now is the clothing, not the footwear. That's a product priority tell. Fashion buyers want the brand for reasons the brand isn't leading with.
Signal: resale markups on apparel outpace shoes on secondary.
SYMPTOM 04
The "knowing" is gone
On used to be an inside bet — the shoe taste-makers quietly wore before it was a thing. That signalling premium has dissolved. Without scarcity or in-the-know entry points, the cultural gatekeepers have no reason to stay.
Signal: the brand isn't in the group chats anymore.
The reframe
"You can't fix brand perception with better ads. You fix it with better objects, better rituals, and better access. The Lab's remit is perception-through-product — exactly what marketing can't touch."
Stop
✕
Celebrity-as-borrowed-taste campaigns. The Zendaya × Drama spot was expensive and cultural-credibility-negative. Do it less.
✕
Mass-market everywhere distribution. Every additional retailer dilutes the cultural premium further. The maths works against you.
✕
Announcing every product launch. Every press release is a nail in the coffin of the "inside bet" feeling.
✕
Positioning the shoe as the hero. For the fashion cohort, apparel is the entry point. Quit forcing the shoes to carry the brand alone.
Start
✓
Product-led credibility. Make objects that critics want to write about because they're beautiful, not because a movie is releasing alongside them.
✓
Designed scarcity. An Unlisted Tier — not in stores, not on the site. Only accessible through ritual (Atelier, OAC, invitation). Hermès logic applied to sportswear.
✓
Silent drops. The fashion cohort finds things. Stop announcing, start placing. Let people discover.
✓
Apparel-first cohort entry. For fashion-forward buyers, lead with apparel, let the shoe follow once they're already inside.
Five Lab-owned moves to rebuild it
01
The Unlisted Line
A permanent tier of On product that exists outside the public catalogue. Not marketed. Not on the website. Only accessible through a named invitation, an Atelier visit, or direct OAC connection. Different materials, restrained silhouettes, small runs. This isn't about price — it's about designed scarcity as a signal. The Hermès Birkin principle applied to craft sportswear. Restores the "knowing" by making something worth knowing about.
Cultural lineage → Hermès · Loewe Paula's Ibiza · Aimé Leon Dore early drops
02
Silent drops
Products that arrive without announcement. A new style simply appears in an Atelier on a Tuesday. No Instagram reel, no press release, no Zendaya. The fashion cohort finds it. By the time mass marketing would have picked it up, the story has already been told by the right people.
Extends → The Living Atelier · 2028
03
Apparel-first entry
For the fashion cohort, lead with clothing — the thing they already like most. Scope a clothing-forward capsule, let the footwear appear alongside as companion, not hero. Reorder the product narrative so shoes ride in on apparel credibility, not the other way around.
Extends → Beyond the Run · On Off vertical
04
Residencies, not collabs
Swap transactional collabs (where a name is stamped on the product) for long residencies. A designer, artist, or architect spends 6–12 months with the Lab. The outcome isn't guaranteed — it might be a shoe, a store, an object, a film. The process itself becomes the content.
Precedent → Prada's Rem Koolhaas residency · Miyake's research studio
05
Hero the athletes, not the celebrities
The casting is the strategy. Zendaya is fashion celebrity borrowing sport. Kipchoge, Obiri, Federer in their actual environments read as earned, not borrowed. The Lab's cultural output should centre the people whose sporting authority is the brand's one true moat.
Extends → The Athlete Protocol
The design principle
The fashion cohort doesn't want to be sold to.
They want to discover something worth keeping.

Every fix above is a variation on one idea: give the taste-makers something to find, rather than something shouted at them. Restraint, scarcity, craft, and silence aren't abstract values — they're the design language of the cultural class On needs to win back. The Lab's job isn't to fix the perception with more noise. It's to design less, better, and more quietly — until the right people start talking about On again without being asked to.

The 2028 bet · The Living Atelier

When retail stops
pretending to be an app.

In 2020, Burberry opened a "Social Retail" store in Shenzhen with Tencent — WeChat-native, QR codes in fitting rooms, a virtual pet called Thomas that levelled up as customers earned social currency. It was first. It was bold. Six years later, it has barely scaled beyond that one store. The concept was right, the execution was wrong. They made a store that tried to be an app.

On has the chance to make the opposite. A store so confident in its craft that the technology disappears into it — where the most luxurious thing isn't the pet, the points, or the fitting-room mirror, but the fact that someone is making your shoe while you watch. That's a shift Burberry, Dior, and even Jacquemus are all moving toward. On can define it.

What didn't work
Burberry · Social Retail, Shenzhen · 2020
Customers unlocked "social currency" by interacting, fed a virtual fawn that levelled up, scanned QR codes in fitting rooms. Tech wrapped around the store rather than embedded in it.
— Gamification cheapens luxury.
— The phone fought the room.
— 6 years on, still just one store.
— PR moment, not a scalable model.
What On can do
The Living Atelier · 2028
A store where 70% of the floor is making. LightSpray machines visible everywhere. Your twin travels with you — when you walk in, your shoe is already being sprayed. Staff greet you by context, not by checkout.
— Craft as the content.
— The phone disappears into the room.
— Memory, not points.
— A model that scales city by city.
01
Ambient twin recognition
Your digital twin arrives before you do. By the time you walk in, the staff know your last run, your next race, your injury history — because you opted in. No tablets, no wristbands. The store just knows.
02
70% making, 30% retail
Flip the ratio. Most of the floor is LightSpray machines, last-builders, and craftspeople working on named pairs. The "product" on display is the act of making itself. Like a Savile Row tailor, not a Nike outlet.
03
Craft as livestream
Every Atelier streams. From bed, you can watch your pair being made. What fashion weeks did once a season, On does every hour. Burberry's fashion show livestream, democratised and intimate.
04
Memory over gamification
No points, no virtual pets, no social currency. Regulars get priority slots, OAC athletes drop by, repeat visitors are remembered by name. Scarcity of attention, not abundance of badges. Relationships compound — gamification doesn't.

The crystallisation · Where does all this lead?

Beyond the shoe.
What On should
actually sell next.

LightSpray was the last five years' value proposition. It won On design awards, press, and a 30x manufacturing scale-up. But technical novelty has a half-life, and the next generation of buyers is already moving on. The question isn't "what's the next shoe?" — it's what's the next value proposition the whole brand can be built around.

DIRECTION 02

This isn't just a product model — it's a whole organising logic. Every section of this deck points to it: the Continuous Pair proves it, hyperpersonalisation makes it work, the Atelier makes it a ritual, Beyond the Run extends it across categories, the Taste Reset protects its scarcity. "The only brand where your first pair is also your last."

Hyperpersonalisation, the Continuous Pair, the Living Atelier, the Beyond-the-Run verticals, the Athlete Protocol, the Taste Reset — none of them are features. They're all facets of one bigger idea: On is the brand where you don't buy a shoe, you enter a relationship. That's the emerging value prop worth betting the Lab on.

04 · The horizons

How I'd structure
the 18+ month bet.

The Lab's mandate is to look 18+ months ahead. That's both a gift and a trap — too far out and the work becomes abstract; too close and it collides with the core roadmap. Here's how I'd split the work across horizons.

0 – 6 months
Foundation
Build the Lab's operating system. Map the existing journey across product, digital, retail, and service. Identify the three most promising intersections where AI actually earns its place. Ship the first testable prototype.
6 – 12 months
Prove the loop
Run the flagship Atelier pilot. Start feeding Lab insight back into the core roadmap quarterly. Establish the Lab as an insight engine — not a tech showroom.
12 – 18 months
Scale what sticks
Promote validated prototypes into core product or retail pipelines. Kill what doesn't resonate. Publish the Lab's first public POV — a signal to the industry and to On's own community.
18+ months
The compounding effect
The Lab becomes a recognised voice inside and outside On. Not a department that produces AI — a way of working that other departments borrow.
