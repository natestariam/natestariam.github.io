01 · How I see On

A brand that already runs
on craft. AI should make it
feel inevitable.

On isn't a tech company trying to add craft. It's a craft company that has quietly built one of the most technically ambitious supply chains in sportswear — LightSpray, CloudTec, 32 robots spraying a shoe upper in three minutes. The story is already radical. The question is what AI changes about how runners, retail partners, and the wider community experience it.

I believe the AI Lab's role isn't to layer AI features onto the product roadmap. It's to protect On's most valuable asset — its sense of taste — while using AI to deepen the relationship between the brand and its community at every touchpoint: the run, the store, the website, the recovery, the next pair.

"The best AI products don't feel like AI. They feel like the brand, turned up. That's what the Lab should be aiming for — not innovation as novelty, but innovation as intimacy."

02 · Four pillars of the Lab's work

Where AI should
earn its place.

01
The Running Self
AI as a mirror for the runner. Not a coach barking metrics, but a quiet reflective layer that helps a runner understand their own patterns, recovery, and progression — and adapts the product experience (fit, cushioning, route recommendations) to who they are today, not who they were last week. The nuance is in restraint: when not to speak.
02
The Physical-Digital Loop
LightSpray makes it possible to manufacture an upper in three minutes. That's a design opportunity most brands don't have. The Lab should prototype what customisation means when production is that fast — in-store experiences where a scan of the foot leads to a shoe sprayed on-demand, with a digital record that evolves with the runner's data over time.
03
Retail as a Stage
On's stores are already beautiful. The Lab should design what happens when they become intelligent — ambient AI that knows regulars, remembers past conversations with staff, and turns the fitting experience into something closer to a made-to-measure appointment. Physical craft meets digital memory.
04
The Quiet Layer
Most AI-driven products are noisy. On's opportunity is to be the opposite — to use AI to remove friction, reduce cognitive load, and give back time. Re-ordering the right pair automatically. Sensing when a runner is injured and proactively offering a lower-impact alternative. The Lab should set the tone: AI as craft, not as spectacle.
03 · Three prototypes I'd scope in the first 90 days

From theory
to artefact.

Strategy is only credible if it leads to something you can touch. These are the three directions I'd want to turn into small, testable prototypes in the first three months — each scoped to validate a different hypothesis about AI's role at On.

Prototype 01
The Second Pair
An AI companion that learns a runner's patterns over the life of their first shoe, and designs the spec of their second — not by recommending a SKU, but by generating the actual LightSpray pattern tailored to the wear data of the first pair. Manufacturing becomes the final design decision, not the first.
Prototype 02
The Quiet Run
A companion app experiment that deliberately says less, not more. Uses real-time sensor data to detect stress, form breakdown, and recovery debt — but only surfaces insight at the moment it's actionable. Tests whether AI can feel restorative rather than demanding.
Prototype 03
The Atelier
A single flagship retail concept: one store, one quarter. Runners walk in, the store knows who they are, a staff member has context on their last run, and by the end of the visit they leave with a shoe sprayed in front of them. Tests the full physical-digital loop as one continuous journey.
Prototype 04
The Community Signal
A tool for On's community managers and OAC athletes that turns community conversation, race data, and product feedback into early signals for R&D. The Lab becomes an insight engine for the rest of the company — not just a prototyping arm.
04 · The horizons

How I'd structure
the 18+ month bet.

The Lab's mandate is to look 18+ months ahead. That's both a gift and a trap — too far out and the work becomes abstract; too close and it collides with the core roadmap. Here's how I'd split the work across horizons.

0 – 6 months
Foundation
Build the Lab's operating system. Map the existing journey across product, digital, retail, and service. Identify the three most promising intersections where AI actually earns its place. Ship the first testable prototype.
6 – 12 months
Prove the loop
Run the flagship Atelier pilot. Start feeding Lab insight back into the core roadmap quarterly. Establish the Lab as an insight engine — not a tech showroom.
12 – 18 months
Scale what sticks
Promote validated prototypes into core product or retail pipelines. Kill what doesn't resonate. Publish the Lab's first public POV — a signal to the industry and to On's own community.
18+ months
The compounding effect
The Lab becomes a recognised voice inside and outside On. Not a department that produces AI — a way of working that other departments borrow.
